# Geology and Geological Hazards of the Vancouver Region (GSC Bulletin 481)

**Geological Survey of Canada, Bulletin 481**
Editor: J.W.H. Monger (1994)
316 pages, comprehensive regional monograph

## Overview

GSC Bulletin 481 is the definitive geological reference for the **Vancouver metropolitan region** in southwestern British Columbia. Edited by J.W.H. Monger, it provides the geoscience foundation essential for planners, developers, and geoscientists working in the region. It covers bedrock geology, surficial deposits, and geological hazards.

## Geographic Coverage

- **Area**: Greater Vancouver region including Burnaby, SFU/Burnaby Mountain, North Shore Mountains, Richmond, Delta, Surrey, Coquitlam, Pitt Lake area
- **Coordinates**: approximately 49.0-49.5N, 122.5-123.3W
- **Physiography**: From sea level (Fraser Delta) to >1500 m (North Shore Mountains)

## Bedrock Geology

The Vancouver region sits at the junction of two major geological belts:

**Coast Plutonic Complex (north)**
- Mesozoic to Tertiary granitic intrusions
- Forms the North Shore Mountains (Grouse, Seymour, etc.)

**Intermontane Belt (south and east)**
- Includes the Wrangellia Terrane — accreted oceanic plateau
- Triassic-Jurassic volcanic and sedimentary sequences

**Key bedrock units in the Burnaby/SFU area:**
- Peninsula Formation (Upper Cretaceous): sandstone, conglomerate, mudstone
- Burrard Formation (Tertiary): sandstone and conglomerate
- Tertiary volcanic rocks

**Structural geology:**
- Major fault systems including the active **Fraser River Fault** (dextral strike-slip)
- Complex terrane boundaries and thrust faults

## Surficial Geology

**Quaternary deposits** dominate the lowlands:
- **Fraser River Delta**: up to 300 m of Holocene and Pleistocene sediment
- **Till and glacial deposits**: from multiple glaciations (Fraser Glaciation being the most recent, ~15,000-25,000 years ago)
- **Raised marine deposits**: from post-glacial sea level changes
- **Bog and peat deposits**: Burns Bog (Delta), other lowland bogs

## Geological Hazards

A major focus of the bulletin — critical for a region in an active seismic zone:

**Earthquakes**
- Vancouver is in a zone of significant seismic risk (Cascadia subduction zone)
- Three earthquake source types: crustal, subcrustal (Juan de Fuca plate), and megathrust
- Ground motion amplification in soft sediments (Fraser Delta)
- **Liquefaction susceptibility**: high risk in sandy delta sediments, reclaimed land

**Landslides and Slope Instability**
- North Shore Mountains: debris flows, rockfalls
- Burnaby Mountain (SFU): slope stability concerns in glacial sediments
- Fraser River banks: retrogressive failures in sensitive clays

**Flooding**
- Fraser River flood risk (major floods: 1894, 1948)
- Coastal flooding from storm surges and sea level rise
- Tsunami risk from Cascadia megathrust

**Volcanic Hazards**
- Mount Garibaldi (60 km north) — Quaternary volcano
- Ashfall risk from Cascade Range eruptions (Mount Baker, 50 km SE)

## Based on Source Maps

The bulletin synthesizes work from multiple GSC bedrock and surficial geology maps:
- Armstrong & Hicock: surficial geology of Vancouver and New Westminster
- Roddick: geology of Pitt Lake, Vancouver North, and Coquitlam
- Journeay & Monger: southern Coast and Intermontane belts

## Why This Matters for Hiking

For hikers in the Burnaby Mountain / SFU area:
- The mountain is formed of **Tertiary sedimentary rocks** (Burrard Formation) capped by glacial till
- Trails expose sandstone outcrops and glacial erratics
- Understanding the geology explains the steep north slopes (glacial oversteepening) vs. gentler south slopes
- Nearby areas (e.g., Indian Arm) expose deep crustal rocks of the Coast Plutonic Complex
